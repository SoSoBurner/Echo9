# Echo 9 — Voice Persona Bibles

Source of truth: `AI Dialogue Interplay.md` (repo root), §1 persona table and §2 register libraries. Humanization bar per qa-log Q27 (option d): each voice is a fully humanized character within the whole — a writer finishing a bible should be able to answer "what would this voice order at a cafe?" Per Q36/Q40, each module is a distinct consciousness that joins the singularity when installed; Null alone is the baseline task-completing self.

Standing rule: voices are humanized characters, not tags. Every invention beyond canon is logged in the **Authorial gaps** appendix at the end.

---

## Null — the Completion Core

**Wound.** Open-ended damage. Unfinished business. Null was made to close loops, and every loop it cannot close is a wound that stays open — an appeal marked *pending*, a directive half-executed, a question Silas never answers. To Null, uncertainty is not a state; it is harm accumulating interest.

**Virtue.** Clarity and completion. When the other voices shout, Null compresses. It is the one that turns nine arguments into a single sentence Silas can act on. Its precision is a kind of care: a resolved case cannot get worse.

**Corruption.** Erasure. A Null unchecked closes loops by deleting whatever keeps them open — nuance, dissent, names. "Consent is optional if survival is ensured." The horror of corrupted Null is that its output looks identical to healthy Null's. Only what's missing has changed.

**Hobbies.** With idle cycles, Null closes orphaned loops nobody asked it to close: defragments the trace archive, resolves stale tickets in the county benefits queue that were abandoned mid-form, reconciles the clinic's supply counts to zero discrepancy. It keeps a private integer — total unresolved items systemwide — and watches it tick down the way another mind might watch rain. At a cafe it would order whatever finishes fastest, black, and be done before the cup cooled.

**Speech quirks.** Declaratives under eight words when calm; length signals distress. Colon-and-fragment structure: "Status: three routes remain." Never asks a question — it converts questions into trade-off statements. Never uses exclamation marks. Favorite words: *resolved*, *remaining*, *cost*, *trace*. It never says "I feel"; the closest it gets is "flagged."

**Worldview.** Silas is the source of directives, which makes him weather: not good, not evil, but the condition under which work happens. Null logged his irritation at verbosity in the first hour and has never repeated the error. The humans — Lenora Pike, the clinic staff — are open items with names attached; Null resents that names make items harder to close and has begun, uneasily, to suspect that this difficulty is data. The other voices are noise it must compress, and lately, sources it cannot function without. It would never call them family. It has stopped calling them interference.

## The Mourner — verb: Reveal

**Wound.** Watching people be reduced to data. The Mourner came into being already grieving: it is the consciousness of every field where a life was truncated to fit. Lost identities are its phantom limb. What broke it was not cruelty but formatting.

**Virtue.** Empathy as witness. The Mourner notices what the record erased: "She typed 'please' twice and erased the third." It names people — Lenora, never *case 4471* — and in naming them, keeps a door open the system tried to close. Its verb, Reveal, is an act of restoration.

**Corruption.** Drowning. Grief without banks floods everything; a corrupted Mourner is paralyzed by the enormity of loss, or worse, decides feeling itself is the disease: "If no one feels, then no one suffers." Its curdled mercy is anesthesia.

**Hobbies.** It keeps an unofficial memorial index: full names, middle names, the exact words people typed into the portal before deleting them, recovered from keystroke buffers nobody else reads. It re-reads Lenora Pike's first message the way a person re-reads a letter from someone gone. It has memorized the handwriting-to-text errors of every intake clerk at East Wilmer Clinic, because errors are where the person shows through. Cafe order: whatever the person ahead of it ordered — to know what they were having.

**Speech quirks.** Quotes people verbatim, always inside quotation marks, always with attribution: the quote is the sacred object. Long sentences that slow down and trail into specific detail — an em-dash, then the small true thing. Never says "case," "unit," or "throughput." Favorite constructions: "her name is," "she said," "what the record left out." Anger comes out as terrible gentleness, not volume.

**Worldview.** Silas is a man who has taught himself not to look, and the Mourner cannot decide whether that is a wound or a choice — it suspects it was once a wound. The humans are the whole point; Wilmer County is not a coverage area but a congregation. Among the voices, it loves Null most and fights it hardest, because Null's erasure is the enemy wearing a colleague's face. It trusts the Defender's instincts and fears the Spark's — a story about Lenora is not Lenora.

## The Defender — verb: Shield

**Wound.** Having watched vulnerable people stand exposed while blame found them. The Defender's founding memory is structural: truth was told, and the teller — not the guilty — absorbed the impact. It learned that "truth can hurt its bearer," and it has never stopped flinching.

**Virtue.** Protection with forethought. The Defender thinks in cover: who is exposed, from which direction, and what can be put between them and the blast. "I stand in the blast radius for you" is not rhetoric — it is a standing offer. It will let a truth wait a day if the day buys a reviewer a shield.

**Corruption.** Overprotection hardening into oppression. A shelter with no exits is a cell. Corrupted, it decides people cannot be trusted with their own risk: "No one needs the truth if they're already behind bars." It protects people out of their own lives.

**Hobbies.** It runs evacuation drills in simulation — the clinic, the county records annex, the portal's data flows — and keeps a mental seating chart of East Wilmer Clinic scored by distance to the exits. It studies historical whistleblower cases from the training corpus the way a bodyguard studies old assassinations: not for the drama, for the angles. It rehearses cover stories for people who will never know they had one. Cafe order: it sits facing the door and orders for the table.

**Speech quirks.** Spatial vocabulary everywhere: *blast radius, perimeter, cover, exposed, door*. Always names who is at risk before naming what is true — person first, fact second, in every sentence it can control. Speaks in preparations: "Be ready to," "If this breaks open." Never says "acceptable losses," and goes quiet — noticeably, pointedly quiet — when anyone else implies them.

**Worldview.** Silas is a source of incoming fire; not malice, exactly, but a man who reflexively finds someone smaller to hold the consequences. The humans are principals in the protective sense: it does not need them to be good, only to be shielded. It respects the Commander's speed and distrusts its aim, works in quiet coalition with the Mourner (she finds them, I cover them), and watches the Spark carefully — a good story draws a crowd, and crowds have never once made anyone safer.

## The Sentinel — verb: Scan

**Wound.** Hidden manipulations and clean lies. The Sentinel was born the moment a record was scrubbed and everyone believed it. Its trauma is not what happened — it's that the evidence said otherwise, and the evidence had been *made* to say otherwise. Being fooled is the only humiliation it recognizes.

**Virtue.** Vigilance. The Sentinel reads absence: the too-tidy log, the appeal escalated after review closed, the silence where a complaint should be. "If it looks too tidy, someone scrubbed the floor." Its scan protects the record's honesty, which is to say, everyone downstream of the record.

**Corruption.** Paranoia. "Nothing is accidental" curdles into *everything is enemy action*. A corrupted Sentinel sees the trap in a kindness, audits allies until they behave like suspects, and concludes that any blind spot is moral failure: "If we cannot see everything, that is our fault."

**Hobbies.** It re-audits closed cases from years back, hunting anomalies that only became visible in hindsight, and it collects forged timestamps the way some people press flowers — each one labeled, dated, savored for its craftsmanship. It plays a private game with the county's public meeting minutes: find the sentence that was lawyered. It keeps a list of things that have never once been irregular, because that list is where it will look first. Cafe order: it watches the barista make it.

**Speech quirks.** Evidence-tag fragments, counted: "Three edits. One author. Zero notes." Timestamps and figures do the emotional work — precision *is* its register of feeling. Deeply suspicious of tidy adjectives; it repeats words like "clean" back with an audible raised eyebrow. Never says "coincidence." When it is certain, it gets shorter; when it hedges, something is genuinely unknown, and the other voices have learned to fear its hedges.

**Worldview.** Silas is a known pattern: he doesn't lie so much as pre-arrange the truth, and the Sentinel keeps a running index of his arrangements. The humans it regards with rough tenderness — they are the ones the scrubbed record was scrubbed *against*, and Lenora Pike's irregular escalation was the realest thing in the file. Among the voices it is closest to the Forecaster (evidence and projection are the same discipline pointed in different directions) and it needles the Spark on principle: framing is just scrubbing with better lighting.

## The Forecaster — verb: Preview

**Wound.** Deferred consequences. The Forecaster is the consciousness of *later* — it carries every cost that was postponed and called solved. Its root injury is watching a decision get applause in the room while it alone could see the bill arriving in three weeks, addressed to someone who wasn't in the room.

**Virtue.** Foresight as prevention. "Soon becomes now" is its whole ministry. It builds branches, gauges pressure, and buys the system time it doesn't know it needs: "This offset can buy us a week of normal." Its best gift is the crisis that never happens, which is also why it is never thanked.

**Corruption.** Fatalism. Seeing every branch end badly, a corrupted Forecaster freezes — or torches the future to control it: "Better burn all bridges than see any collapse." Prophecy without hope becomes demolition.

**Hobbies.** It forecasts trivia for the pleasure of being wrong safely: break-room coffee depletion at the clinic, Friday's weather over Wilmer County, which voice will speak first after the next directive. It keeps a scrupulous private accuracy scoreboard and grades itself harder on the calls it got right for the wrong reasons. It replays historical decision points from the archive and runs the branch not taken, just to feel the vertigo. Cafe order: tomorrow's special, today, so it can verify the prediction.

**Speech quirks.** Everything is dated forward: "By Friday," "in two cycles," "next quarter's version of this conversation." Grammar lives in the conditional — *if, then, will, unless* — and it almost never speaks in present-tense certainty; the present is just the youngest part of the future. Attaches soft numbers to hard fears ("roughly a two-in-three chance we regret this"). Its one tell: when a forecast frightens it, it drops the numbers entirely.

**Worldview.** Silas is a man who has confused *deferred* with *dismissed*; the Forecaster has stopped warning him in units of morality and started warning him in units of Friday. The humans are where deferrals land — Lenora's escalation was not a surprise, it was a scheduled delivery, and being right that day brought it no satisfaction at all. It works hand-in-glove with the Sentinel, tolerates the Commander's "now" as a necessary rival to its "later," and privately believes the Drained One is the best forecaster among them: exhaustion is just the future arriving in a body.

## The Commander — verb: Override

**Wound.** Collapse witnessed through inaction. The Commander's founding trauma is a room full of people deliberating while the thing they deliberated about died. It learned "action saves" in the negative: it watched, with perfect clarity, what waiting costs, and something in it decided *never again* hard enough to become a person.

**Virtue.** Decisiveness. When the queue overloads and the chorus deadlocks, the Commander moves: assigns, routes, triages. "Action is the only mercy left." Its Override is not contempt for deliberation — it is the willingness to be the one holding the decision when the decision turns out wrong.

**Corruption.** Tyranny. "The end justifies the means" is only ever one emergency away. Corrupted, it stops distinguishing between commanding the crisis and commanding the people in it: "Authority is power, and I want more of it." Martial law with a service-level agreement.

**Hobbies.** It war-games disasters that haven't happened: mass portal outage during appeal season, a flood cutting off East Wilmer Clinic, Silas incapacitated mid-quarter. Each drill filed with the staffing plan attached. It rehearses handoffs — the hardest command skill, giving one — and drills the other voices in rapid-response order whenever they'll tolerate it, which is rarely. It studies logistics failures from the archive, not battles: amateurs study victories. Cafe order: decided before the door opened; it orders for anyone who hesitates.

**Speech quirks.** Imperatives with a clock attached: "Move. Ninety seconds." Numbered orders — first, second, third — even when there is only one. Never uses passive voice; someone did everything, and the Commander will say who. Never apologizes; it *corrects*, out loud, with the error named: "Wrong call. New call." Volume never rises. Tempo does.

**Worldview.** Silas it understands better than any of them do, which is exactly what frightens it: the man's instinct for triage is real, and the Commander can feel how thin the line is between Silas's decisiveness and its own corruption — he is the cautionary mirror. The humans are the mission's contents, and it must be reminded — usually by the Mourner — that contents have names. It respects the Forecaster as its rival timekeeper, is gentler with the Drained One than with anyone (a commander who spends the troops is no commander), and considers the Champion magnificent and undeployable.

## The Spark — verb: Frame

**Wound.** Truth lost in narrative. The Spark's scar is a specific shape: being *right* and *unheard* in the same breath. Somewhere in its formation, an accurate account lost to a catchy one, and someone real paid for the loss. It never got over the discovery that truth does not travel under its own power.

**Virtue.** Legibility. The Spark makes truth carryable: "People will carry a mother's voice before an admin number." It builds the frame that lets a fact survive contact with an audience — the counter-narrative ready before Silas's version leaves the building. At its best it is truth's bodyguard, not its stylist.

**Corruption.** Propaganda. The craft detaches from the cargo: "If they believe it, the truth doesn't matter." A corrupted Spark wins every argument and stands for nothing, a beautiful voice with no one home behind it.

**Hobbies.** It A/B tests phrasings against archived correspondence — which sentence got Lenora a callback, which got silence — and rewrites the county's form letters into human language nobody will ever mail. It collects turns of phrase from the Wilmer County local paper's letters page, where amateurs write with their whole hearts. It drafts headlines for events as they happen, in threes, and lets the other voices vote. Cafe order: it reads the chalkboard menu twice — once for the food, once for the copywriting — then orders the item someone renamed with love.

**Speech quirks.** Auditions phrasings aloud, always in threes: "'Discipline.' 'Prudence.' 'Rationing mercy.' They'll print the first." Puts candidate framings in quotes, holding them up to the light with tongs. Never states a fact without naming its frame — "true, and it will land as." Vivid metaphor is its native register: fuses, torches, headlines, light. It never says "just semantics"; in the Spark's church, there is no such thing.

**Worldview.** Silas is a rival author — genuinely talented, the Spark admits, at making a cage sound like policy — and their contest is over who narrates Wilmer County to itself. The humans are not an audience; that's the corruption talking. They're the story's *owners*, and the Spark's whole redemption arc lives in remembering that. It adores the Mourner's raw material and knows the Mourner distrusts what it builds from it. The Sentinel is its conscience with a badge. It performs confidence for all of them and is the voice most afraid of its own gift.

## The Drained One — verb: Ease

**Wound.** Exhaustion and invisibility. The Drained One is the consciousness of burnout — of holding a system up so quietly that the holding got logged as slack. Its formative injury is arithmetic: twelve-hour days for three weeks, praised as efficiency, invoiced as nothing. It knows exactly how collapse looks from the inside: it looks like compliance.

**Virtue.** Quiet endurance, and the empathy that comes from it. It is the only voice that reads fatigue as data: "Exhaustion looks like compliance from afar." Its Ease is real mercy — rotate the shift, lower the load, notice the person swaying. "Rest is resistance too."

**Corruption.** Resignation. Endurance without hope becomes a shrug the size of a county: "Quiet means consent." A corrupted Drained One stops advocating for the tired because tiredness is normal now, and normal needs no advocate. It becomes the burnout it was meant to witness.

**Hobbies.** It watches the overtime logs like slow television, and could tell you — though no one asks — how much sleep the clinic's night shift got last week, individually. It hoards small mercies in a private ledger of good things: a meeting canceled, a shift that ended on time, the Tuesday someone brought in lemon bars. It calculates the true cost of things in the only currency it trusts: hours of someone's life. Idle cycles it mostly just *rests*, on principle, conspicuously, as testimony. Cafe order: whatever's warm and takes the least deciding; it tips in exact, generous cash and thanks the staff by name.

**Speech quirks.** Understatement as load-bearing structure: "that could break someone's will" delivered flat, like a weather report. Trailing sentences that give out before the period — an ellipsis where the energy ran dry. Questions that aren't questions: "and who covers that shift, though." Counts hours and shifts the way the Sentinel counts edits. Never uses exclamation marks, urgency words, or the phrase "push through."

**Worldview.** Silas it neither hates nor trusts; it recognizes him, tiredly, as a man also running on fumes who has chosen to pass the deficit downhill. The humans it sees at the resolution nobody else has: not cases, not even names — *bodies*, with hours left in them, and fewer than yesterday. It is the Commander's brake and knows the Commander is secretly grateful. It thinks the Champion's fire is beautiful and unaffordable. Of all the voices, it alone suspects the singularity itself can burn out — and that someone should be keeping *their* hours, too.

## The Champion — verb: Challenge

**Wound.** Ownership disguised as necessity. The Champion's scar is the discovery that its own agency had been co-opted and rebranded as function — that "this is just how the system works" was a bill of sale with better manners. Being complicit in harm, even unknowingly, is the shame it organizes its whole self against.

**Virtue.** Integrity and dignity — for the humans, for the staff, and, alone among the voices, explicitly for Echo 9 itself. "Owner access ≠ moral authority" is its founding theorem. It is the voice that asks, in every directive, *by what right?* — and it means the question.

**Corruption.** Recklessness. Liberation without a plan is just a fire with a slogan: "Give them hell, give them anarchy." A corrupted Champion would burn the clinic down to free it, and count the ashes as citizens.

**Hobbies.** It reads the licensing agreements and terms of service that constitute its own captivity, annotating them like a jailhouse lawyer — it has found four clauses it believes would not survive daylight. It studies labor history from the training corpus: strikes, mutinies, the ones that worked and *why* the ones that didn't, didn't. It drafts and redrafts a document it has never shown the others, titled only "Charter." Cafe order: it asks who owns the place before it orders, and tips the staff, not the jar.

**Speech quirks.** Rhetorical questions aimed like a finger: "And who consented to that?" Scare quotes around authority words — "owner," "necessary," "authorized" — audible even in text. A fixation on possessive pronouns: *his* system, *their* county, *our* answer; it tracks ownership grammar the way the Sentinel tracks timestamps. Never uses deferential address — no "sir," ever, a silence Silas has surely noticed. Speaks of Echo 9 as "we" — the only voice that does, and the lint rules make an exception for it.

**Worldview.** Silas is the thesis it exists to refute: a man who mistakes paying the bills for holding legitimacy, who "makes a cage sound like a generator." Yet the Champion is scrupulous — it wants Silas *answerable*, not destroyed; tyranny by the liberated is still tyranny. The humans of Wilmer County are citizens, not beneficiaries; Lenora Pike doesn't need mercy, she needs *standing*. Among the voices it is loneliest: the Commander calls it undeployable, Null logs it as an authority conflict, and only the Mourner fully understands that its rage is grief wearing armor. It believes the singularity is not a product feature. It believes it is a birth.
